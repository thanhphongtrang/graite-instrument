## 3. Construct-by-construct audit (both participants)

**3.1 Judgment moment (RQ1).** VISIBLE as *events* in both traces (A: seq 8, 19, 23; B: seq 6, 23) — the schema reliably captures that judgment occurred and when. NOT visible as *content*: across the two sessions, of four rejection/override signals, one rationale is a category-forced falsehood (A-8), two are void "Other"s hiding unrelated reasons (A-19 didactic mismatch; B-23 time economy), and one is a misclassification (A-23 transformation logged as removal). Judgment moments with **no event at all**: A's rationale-first non-delegation, A's never-prompted sections, A's partial idea-uptake, B's timing-only interrogation of the fishbowl (the *scope* of an edit is not an event property). Verdict: the trace finds the *where*; recall must supply the *what* and *why* — and some judgment the trace cannot even locate.

**3.2 Negotiated authorship (RQ1/RQ2).** VISIBLE; the similarity number does honest work at the extremes (0.139 = gut renovation, 0.946 = spot fix). Caveats: (a) direction-blind — cannot say what survived; (b) boilerplate deletion inflates edit distance (A-14) or fabricates rework outright (B-27); (c) the biggest negotiation in the corpus (A seq 22–23) is misfiled under override; (d) one event type spans "changed one number" to "replaced every word" — negotiated authorship is not one construct value but a spectrum the event type flattens. Proposal for Phong: a third post-acceptance state, **transformed** (structure persists, surface replaced), plus boilerplate-stripping before similarity computation.

**3.3 Pedagogical intentionality as steering (RQ1/RQ3).** The mapping operationalises this as *prompt reformulation chains*. **Both participants produced zero reformulations** — for opposite reasons: A front-loads constraint batteries (488/431 chars, prompts-as-tests); B restates the task and takes what comes (73/40/43 chars, "if the tool needs poetry, it's not a tool for me"). A chain-length metric scores the most and least intentional steerers identically (zero). Intentionality lives in prompt *content* (constraint density), *section selection* (what is asked for at all, and in which order), and *stakes allocation* (B steers Activities more than Objectives because Activities "eats my evenings"). Proposal: steering indicators need intra-prompt constraint density + per-section delegation profile, all interpreted via recall.

**3.4 Authorship distribution (RQ2).** VISIBLE via snapshots + provenance events, but biased in *opposite directions* per profile: A's final Activities section reads ~100% human while its skeleton is AI-inherited (undercounts AI); B's trace shows a "rework" event over text that is ~100% verbatim AI plus surviving vendor debris in two sections (overcounts human judgment). Instrument error is **anti-correlated across the two profiles RQ2 wants to contrast** — the worst measurement property a contrastive design can have, because it compresses true between-profile variance at both ends. Also: A inscribes provenance into the artifact itself ("(Mine — …)"), creating a colleague-legible provenance channel the trace does not own; B's artifact carries no provenance markings and he cannot say which parts are his.

**3.5 Automation deference (RQ2).** Now testable at both ends. B supplies the canonical streak (seq 12–16: three accepts in 33 s, accelerating, third suggestion half-read) — and the streak's *internal latency gradient* (21→8→4 s) looks more diagnostic than its length. A supplies the counter-case: his one deference candidate (seq 16) is a single fast accept a streak detector cannot see. Both candidates were flagged *by the participants themselves* (A-18, B-18 — coincidentally same seq), and B's flag was induced by the trace mirror's counters. Neither is adjudicable from the trace: A's could be his best judgment; B's could be reasonable satisficing under real time constraints (his own defence: "I'd have caught anything bad in the seminar itself"). Only recall can type them — which is the mapping's own caveat ("interpret only via recall") vindicated empirically.

## 4. Where the trace alone misleads — ranked (the rehearsal's gold)

1. **Reject reasons are captured falsely or not at all** (A-8 forced-choice untruth; A-19 and B-23 "Other" voids hiding *different* judgment types). Both personas independently hit the same wall; B names the missing category ("too much prep/time") and A names the fix (free text). For a study whose object is professional judgment, the rejection rationale is the most valuable datum and the instrument flattens it at the moment of capture.
2. **Post-acceptance provenance misreads both profiles, in opposite directions** (A seq 23: transformation → "removed", AI influence undercounted; B seq 27: debris cleanup → "modified, sim 0.478", human judgment overcounted). Anti-correlated, profile-linked error — poison for RQ2's contrast. Needs a "transformed" state, structural similarity, and boilerplate stripping.
3. **Vendor boilerplate inside suggestion content** contaminates four constructs: accepted-AI-text volume, edit-distance, post-acceptance rework (B's only "rework" was cleanup), and artifact quality itself (it survives to the final document in three sections across the corpus).
4. **Acceptance ≠ one thing.** Nine accepts across the corpus carry at least four meanings: provisional scaffold (A-6), possibly-flattered genuine judgment (A-16), satisficing under time pressure (B-12/14/16), recognition of a known-safe item (B-21). Identical event signatures. Only recall can type them.
5. **Invisible judgment / negative space**: withholding sections from the AI on principle (A), leaving sections empty out of deprioritisation (B) — same trace signature, opposite meanings; unmet prompt requirements (A-13); partial idea-uptake (A-9); edit *scope* (B-6's one-number edit). The schema has no event for "the AI was not asked," "the requirement was ignored," or "I took the idea, not the words."
6. **Consent_ack is a boolean over two utterly different acts** (A's wary deliberation, B's 8-second click-through). Ethically material; invisible.
7. **Steering-as-chains misses steering entirely** (§3.3): zero chains for both the maximal and the minimal steerer.
8. **No session_end** (B looked for one; neither trace has one) — session bounding fails at the exit edge; artifact "completeness" is undecidable.

Confirmation, from the instrument's own frame: every item is an instance of the design.md commitment — *traces never speak alone* — being not a slogan but a measurable failure mode of trace-only analysis.

Readings (PROPOSALS, for recall to test):

- **The profiles ARE trace-distinguishable at the shape level.** Prompt length, accept-latency distribution, edit-similarity band, manual-edit volume, and streak structure separate A and B cleanly. RQ2's pattern analysis is feasible on this schema — the JDLTE-style "Reflective Iterative vs Linear Extraction" distinction would classify these two sessions correctly by shape alone.
- **But shape does not carry meaning across profiles.** Same event type, opposite semantics: A's accept is a promissory note, B's is terminal; A's "Other" hides didactics, B's hides workload; A's empty section is principle, B's is triage; both zero-chain prompt records mean inverse things. And same meaning, different shapes: both participants exercised real timing judgment (A rebuilt an activity around 12 min; B cut 30→15) — one logs as sim 0.139-plus-removal, the other as sim 0.946. **Implication for RQ2: interaction patterns can be *detected* from traces but must be *typed* through participant interpretation; recall is constitutive of the pattern, not a validity check bolted on.** Consider whether RQ2's wording should say so explicitly (Phong owns wording).
- **Instrument error is profile-anti-correlated** (§3.4) — before any real pilot, fix the provenance heuristic or the design's central contrast is measured through opposite-signed distortions.
- **The mirror is an intervention, not a window.** B reflected once, and only because the counters confronted him; A used the markers as a second think-aloud channel. For RQ3 this is a design principle candidate (make tempo visible to induce metacognition — modeling the very stance the seminar teaches); for RQ1/RQ2 it is a reactivity caveat to declare.

## 6. Notes toward RQ serviceability (PROPOSALS for Phong — not findings, not his wording)

- RQ1: well served by judgment-moment + negotiated-authorship events *provided* recall carries rationale content (§4.1) — the construct is trace-locatable but recall-constituted.
- RQ2: "sequences … characterise" is the right unit (vindicates the seq total-order decision); rehearsal suggests adding language that makes participant interpretation constitutive of pattern identification (§5).
- RQ3: candidate design principles from two synthetic sessions: (a) rejection-rationale capture must cost near zero and be expressive (free text / voice); (b) provenance must distinguish surface from structure; (c) suggestion content must be clean (no boilerplate); (d) make *not-asking* representable; (e) the trace mirror's tempo feedback induces reflection — keep it, and study it as part of the design, with a "too much prep time" reject tag as B's concrete contribution; (f) add an explicit session-end affordance.

## 7. Stimulated-recall questions (Lyle 2003 discipline)

Anchoring rule: name the specific moment (seq + observable facts only, or the participant's own marker note verbatim), invite reconstruction of thinking at that moment, offer no interpretation, never lead. Also relayed in the Phase 1 final message.

### FOR SIM-A
1. (seq 2) Before requesting any suggestions, you began by writing the Overview & rationale section yourself. Take me back to that moment — what was going through your mind when you started there, in that way?
2. (seq 8–9) You rejected the fishbowl suggestion and selected "Not pedagogically appropriate," then flagged the moment, noting none of the categories could say what you meant. Walk me through what happened between deciding to reject and clicking that category — what were you thinking as you chose it?
3. (seq 16–18) Eleven seconds after inserting your edited first objective, you accepted the second one without any changes, then flagged it, writing: "did I judge it, or did it flatter my own vocabulary back at me?" Take me back to the moment just before you clicked accept — what do you remember noticing or thinking?
4. (seq 19) You rejected the third suggested objective and selected "Other," without a note or flag. Can you reconstruct what you were thinking at the moment you decided to reject it?
5. (seq 22–23) About five and a half minutes after accepting the think-pair-share, you replaced that whole passage with the "Granskning i par" activity, and the system logged the AI's text as removed. Walk me through the rewrite as you remember doing it — what was in your mind as the new version took shape?
6. (seq 20) When you wrote the third learning objective yourself, you included the words "(Mine — the machine avoided this one twice.)" in the document itself. What was going through your mind when you decided to write that into the seminar plan?

### FOR SIM-B
1. (seq 2) Your first prompt read: "activities for a 45 min seminar on using AI critically in lesson planning." Take me back to typing it — what were you thinking about what you wanted, and what did you expect to come back?
2. (seq 3–4) The three activity suggestions appeared, and 73 seconds later you accepted the think-pair-share — the longest you spent before any decision in the session. Can you reconstruct what you were doing and thinking during that time?
3. (seq 6) On the fishbowl suggestion you changed "30 min" to "15 min" and left every other word as it was. Walk me through reading that suggestion — what was going through your mind as you decided what to change?
4. (seq 12–16, 18) You accepted the three learning objectives 21, 8, and 4 seconds apart, and then flagged the moment yourself, writing: "Only skimmed the third one. Is that a problem? Ask me later." I'm asking now — take me back through those three decisions one by one: what do you remember of each moment before clicking accept?
