2. 4. The first one I actually read — "Lgr22" did a lot of the persuading, because curriculum-anchored is what the course board wants to see, and 21 seconds is about my honest reading speed for two sentences before a yes. The third one — I have to be straight with you — I remember the shape of it, "pupils compare AI and textbook," and the feeling that three objectives makes a complete-looking set, and that's it. Four seconds is a pattern-match, not a decision. That's exactly what nagged at me enough to flag it: not that the objective was bad, but that I couldn't have told you what it said. 6. I'd just finished the Materials section and was scrolling back down the document — the first time I actually looked at the artifact as a whole rather than at the suggestion cards. My only thought was the projector: student teachers seeing that line and knowing exactly how this seminar got written. So I deleted it, one line, ten seconds. I didn't go looking for the other copies — I know now they're still sitting in Objectives and Materials, which tells you the cleanup was about the section I could see, not a proofreading pass.

## SIM-A — Round 2 stimulated-recall interview (returning veteran, second session, knows the coding)

1. (seq 8, the spelling edit) I remember the cursor sitting between the two buttons and thinking: if I accept this clean, seq 16 happens again — another eleven-second acceptance sitting in your log like evidence of deference. So I opened the editor, changed judgment to judgement — my spelling, the one I have used since Umeå — and inserted. Let me be honest about the order of operations: the doubt was not about the sentence, which I had already tested for two weeks in my head and found sound; the doubt was about how my clicking would *read*. That is the uncomfortable part: your instrument now measures a man managing his own record, and a receipt is not a judgment — it is a signature forged in the shape of one. If participants leave receipts, your edit-rate stops measuring scrutiny and starts measuring self-consciousness, and you will need the interview to tell you which is which — the trace alone cannot.

2. (seq 14–16, detector) Your similarity arithmetic reads words, and words are precisely what I replace; the skeleton is not made of words. Concretely: look for preserved *structure*, not preserved strings. The AI's suggestion had a sequence — encounter an AI artifact, annotate it with keep/revise/reject, then defend the annotations socially — and my Granskning i par keeps every joint of that sequence: same artifact-annotation-defence order, same three-way verdict scheme, same pair-based accountability, different timing, different words, added safeguards. A detector could parse both texts into steps — actor, action, object, sequence position — and compare the graphs; mine maps onto the machine's almost one-to-one. Even cruder would work: the keep/revise/reject triad survives verbatim as a *category system*, and category systems are the most load-bearing thing a suggestion can contribute. Count those inheritances, and stop telling me that the better I teach the more I deleted.

3. (seq 4, capitals) Yes, it has changed, and I will not pretend the capitals were anything but learned behaviour: session one taught me, in the trace's own black and white, that this machine drops the hardest clause in any brief, so I shouted the clause. In the seminar room I do the same with a student who has twice not heard me — repetition, emphasis, a raised finger — so in one sense I am simply treating the machine as the poor listener it has documented itself to be. Whether that is contamination or subject matter depends on what you claim to study: if your object is "the naive first encounter," I am spoiled goods; if your object is how professional judgment *adapts* to a tool over time — and for teacher education it had better be — then the capitals are a finding, not a flaw. Teachers do not meet these tools once. The adaptation is the phenomenon.

4. (consent) The first reading is consent to something imagined; the second is consent to something *experienced* — by then I had seen my own hesitations turned into rows, so each bullet had a face. A multi-session study should therefore re-consent lightly but genuinely at each session: not the same wall of text, but a short delta — "since last time, here is what your trace was used for, here is what changed in what we record" — the way I would tell a class what I did with their evaluations. The v0.2 changes actually deserved this: you altered what the instrument captures partly *because of* what I said, and I should have been asked to acknowledge that, not merely benefit from it. And show the participant their own previous trace before the new session begins, officially, rather than leaving it as something I happened to have seen. Consent that does not age with the study is decoration.

5. (mirror or stage) Both, and I dislike that the honest answer is both. It is a mirror in that seq 14 caught your detector mislabelling me and I could object in the moment — that is transparency doing real work, and I would not give it back. But it is a stage in that seq 8 exists at all: I performed a spelling edit for an audience of one researcher, and a mirror does not make you perform. Session-five-me would be fluent in this instrument — receipts polished, flags eloquent, prompts pre-shouted — so no, he is not session-one-me, and neither of them is the man planning a seminar alone on a Sunday with no log running. Trust neither session as "the truth"; trust the *trajectory* — what changes across sessions is where the honest signal lives, because the drift itself is what your instrument does to people. And budget your interviews accordingly: the later the session, the more the trace needs me to translate it.

## SIM-D — Round 3 stimulated-recall interview (mathematics-didactics specialist, first session, prompt-blinded)

1. (seq 3–4, accepting "Error-hunt"; seq 9 recall_marker) Honestly? In the moment it felt like a clean pedagogical call, but sitting with your question, I have to admit the two things were tangled together and I probably couldn't have separated them cleanly even if I'd tried. What I was consciously weighing was: does this activity make students actually *do* something with the AI output rather than just receive it — yes, they have to hunt for a specific error and defend a claim, that's real mathematical argumentation, not just "reflect on AI." But you're right to press on this, because the reason it registered as "yes" so fast is exactly that it used my own framing back at me — "worked solution," "hunt," the whole shape of my prompt. A suggestion that echoes your own words is going to clear your mental bar faster than one that doesn't, regardless of whether it's actually the better activity. I don't think I was fooled, exactly — I still think error-hunt is pedagogically sound — but I didn't do the work of asking "is this better than what I'd have thought of myself," I just asked "is this what I asked for," and those aren't the same question. That's a little uncomfortable to admit.

2. (seq 6, rejecting "Fishbowl discussion", tag "Too much preparation or time") It wasn't really about the fishbowl idea being weak — if anything the framing question ("if an AI can generate a valid proof, what's left for the pupil to understand?") is sharper than most of what I come up with unprompted. It was purely a capacity call: I'd already spent my one meaty activity slot on the error-hunt, and thirty more minutes of fishbowl on top of a twenty-minute error-hunt plus a representation add-on doesn't fit a normal seminar length — I'd be cutting something else to make room, and nothing else felt cuttable. The tip wasn't "this is bad," it was "this is good but it's a different session's worth of content." Noting I might reuse it later wasn't politeness to the AI — it was literally me parking an idea in my own head the way I'd park a colleague's suggestion in a meeting I didn't have time to act on.

3. (seq 7, edited-then-inserted representation exercise, visible splice in the edited text) What I was doing was trying to shrink and localize the suggestion — make it a shorter add-on instead of a 25-minute stand-alone piece, and swap the vague "a concept" for something concrete, derivative-as-rate-of-change, since abstract examples don't land well with student teachers who need something they can picture teaching. I selected what I thought was the whole suggestion text and typed my replacement over it. I did not notice anything wrong at the moment I typed it — the edit-then-insert flow just took my typed text and moved on, and visually the "Edit first" box didn't make it obvious that only part of the selection had actually been replaced. I only caught it afterward, when I went back into the Activities section to do the fuller rewrite and read through what was actually sitting there — that's when I saw my new sentence sitting stitched into the middle of the old one, half of the original suggestion still trailing after it. It wasn't a dramatic "oh no" moment, more a "wait, that's not what I wrote" double-take, followed by having to fix it as part of the larger rewrite anyway.

4. (seq 10, manual_edit — full rewrite of Activities after suggestions already inserted) Two things, really, and I want to be honest that they're not fully separable. First, practically: once I noticed the splice from the representation-exercise edit, I had to go back in and clean that up regardless — I wasn't going to leave garbled text in a document I'm putting in front of student teachers. But second, and this is just how I work regardless of whether anything had gone wrong: I don't trust AI-authored sentences to carry my voice, even when I've already said "yes, use this." Accepting a suggestion is a decision about the *content* — is this the right activity — but it doesn't settle how it should *sound*, and by the time I'm looking at it sitting in my own seminar plan next to sections I've written myself, the AI phrasing always reads slightly off-register to me — a bit generic, a bit like it's talking about "a concept" instead of committing to derivative-as-rate-of-change specifically. So even without the splice, I think I'd have gone back in. The splice just gave me an immediate practical reason to open the box; the deeper reason was that I hadn't yet made it mine.

5. (seq 12, second prompt recorded as a merged/garbled string) I intended to type a completely fresh sentence — the one about learning objectives, "beyond 'can use AI tools,'" naming the critical stance, pitched at teacher-education level rather than a coding bootcamp. What I did not intend was for any part of my first prompt (the proof-problem one from the Activities cycle) to still be sitting in that box. I clicked into the prompt field, and I genuinely believed I was typing into an empty field, the way it looked the first time I used it. I did not notice anything was wrong in the moment — I typed, hit "ask for suggestions," and the suggestions that came back were good enough and on-topic enough that I didn't think to scroll back and re-read my own prompt critically. It was only afterward, going back through what actually got recorded, that I saw the two prompts had merged into one run-on Frankenstein sentence, with my first prompt's ending grafted onto my second prompt's middle. That's a more unsettling realization on reflection than it was at the time — in the moment I felt fine; only in hindsight do I realize the box couldn't be trusted to represent what I asked, silently.

6. (seq 14–15, provenance_self_report fired twice; seq 21 recall_marker on the double-fire) It felt a little like being asked the same question twice by someone who'd forgotten they'd already asked — my first instinct was "didn't I just answer this?" But once I looked at what each one was actually attached to, it made sense: one was about the error-hunt suggestion I'd accepted outright, the other about the representation exercise I'd edited-then-inserted — two separate pieces of AI text, even though from where I was sitting it was all just "the thing I did to Activities" in one continuous stretch of effort. The options themselves fit fine, both times, no forcing needed — "its ideas, wrote it my way" is exactly what happened to both of those texts, since I kept the underlying pedagogical moves (hunt for a reproduced misconception; notice a missing representation) but essentially none of the original sentences. What I'm less sure about is whether the tool should have bundled those into one question — "you touched two things in Activities, tell me about both" — instead of firing twice back to back, because as a user experience it read as a glitch before it read as two legitimate, distinct questions.

7. (seq 19, third learning-objective suggestion, edited to "quietly skipped") "Quietly skipped" over the original's more clinical "the AI path skips" — that wasn't just style-polishing, it was me trying to smuggle in the actual point I wanted the objective to make. The original phrasing states a neutral fact: the AI path skips certain practices. But the thing I actually care about, the thing this whole seminar is built around, is that the AI does this *without flagging it* — a pupil or a student teacher reading a slick AI-generated solution has no cue that anything's missing unless they already know to look. "Quietly" carries that invisibility, that the omission is silent rather than announced, which is precisely the danger I want them to be alert to. It's a small word, but it's doing the work of turning a descriptive objective into one with a bit of an edge — closer to the critical stance I was asking for in my prompt in the first place, and closer to how I'd actually say it out loud to a room of student teachers.
